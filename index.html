Congo Rainforest

Where is the Congo Rainforest?

The Congo rainforest is situated in the central & western part of tropical Africa. It is the second largest rainforest and makes up approximately 18% of the world’s remaining tropical rainforest. The rainforest has a coastline to the Atlantic ocean and is bordered with six countries such as Democratic Republic of the Congo, Gabon, Cameroon, Central African Republic and Congo. The Congo River, the world's second largest river, passes through the rainforest.

Climate

Seasons

The Congo Rainforest experiences a tropical climate all year, with abundant rainfall and high humidity and temperatures. In regions above the equator, the dry season lasts from November to March, and the rainy season lasts from April to October. In the south, it's the other way around.

Temperatures

The average temperature is quite consistent throughout the year, with just minor seasonal changes. Temperatures can range from the 15° to 27° C. The temperature difference between day and night can be as great as 27 degrees Celsius.

Rainfall

The rainforest receives a lot of rain each year, approximately 1220 to 2032 mm on a yearly basis. According to a recent study, the dry season in the Congo rainforest has been growing longer for the past 25 years. If the dry season continues to lengthen, the forest's existing vegetation may transform into drought-tolerant species.

Why Is this Ecosystem Special?

The Congo rainforest occupies 1.5 million square miles in the African continent. It is known for its outstanding level of biodiversity, including over 600 tree species and 10,000 animal species. More than 400 other mammal species, 1,000 bird species and 700 species of fish can be found here. About 11,000 plants have been identified in the Congo rainforest and 10% are unique to the region. It is found that the Congo rainforest’s soil and plants store incredible amounts of carbon – 8%, stopping it from being released into our atmosphere and fuelling climate change. The Democratic Republic of the Congo's forests are reported to be the world's fourth-largest terrestrial carbon reservoir. There are six national parks in the Congo Rainforest that are World Heritage Sites (meaning they have special significance to the world). It is one of the most significant wilderness areas left on Earth. It’s high level of biodiversity makes it a vibrant ecosystem that is always teeming with life.

Abiotic/Biotic Factors

Biotic Factors

Biotic factors are living organisms that have an effect on other living things or the environment in an ecosystem. They are often categorised as the producers, consumers and decomposers. Some are:

- Animals: are consumers who potentially eat other consumers and depend on producers for some of their food. Their presence creates the biodiverse nature in the rainforest. Animals that live in the Congo rainforest are forest elephants, leopards, okapis, hippos, lions and gorillas.

- Plants: are the prime producers of the rainforest. They convert water, sunlight and carbon dioxide into carbohydrates and provide astonishing support for a range of life. Some found in the Congo rainforest are. Trees such as the African oak, red cedar, walnut trees, mahogany tree and coconut palms.

- Insects: are involved in a broad spectrum of trophic interactions with other organisms. There are a numerous variety of insects found in the Congo rainforest such as bees, grasshoppers, caterpillars, praying mantises, beetles, dragonflies, scorpions, mosquitos, tsetse flies, ants, termites, spiders and many more.

Abiotic Factors

Abiotic factors are non-living components of an ecosystem that influence its environment. The lithosphere, atmosphere, and hydrosphere are the most common sources of abiotic factors. Examples are:

- Water: all living organisms depend on water and need it to survive and reproduce.

- Soil: the Congo rainforest and every other rainforest depends on soil for its nutrients because majority of plants grow in soil. It is a major source of nitrogen, phosphorus and potassium.

- Sunlight: like water, sunlight is a crucial element in the growth of plants. If there was no sunshine, plants wouldn’t be able to photosynthesis.

- Carbon dioxide: this is an essential part of the cycle of life for plants. In the process of ‘photosynthesis’, plants use light energy – sunlight – to turn C02 and water into sugar and oxygen

Symbiosis

Mutualism...

is the relationship between two different kinds of organisms where both of them benefit.

- A fascinating mutual relationship is between crocodiles and plovers. After a meal, the crocodile will position itself comfortably on the riverbank and sit with its mouth wide open. This action is a sign for the little bird to reach into the crocodile’s mouth and pick off any remaining food left in the reptile’s massive teeth. As a result, the tiny birds receive a free meal while the crocodile receives a free dental check up to prevent any infection from raw meat. If, during the process, the plover detects threat from another animal, it will fly away and alert the crocodile to dash into the water and hide from any possible danger.

Commensalism...

is the interaction between species in which only one species benefits but the other is not harmed.

- One of the most common relationships in nature is between the birds and the trees. The birds presence generally does not bring harm to the tree, but it does provide shelter and protection for the bird.

Parasitism...

is when one species (the parasite) benefits and the other species is harmed.

- The strangler fig begins its life as a leaf on a tree branch. It winds around the tree and rises downwards towards the ground and upwards to the sky. After enveloping the tree, the strangler fig kills it by stealing sunlight and root space.
